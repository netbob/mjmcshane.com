Quaoar lifted his bow and sighted to his target; a massive black bear; piyahdonaht as his people called it. He knew he had to make a perfect shot and pierce the creature's heart. Quaoar released the arrow and it sped thirty yards to the Bears’ upper rear shoulder. Quaoar and the bear made eye contact and as the life went out of the bears’ eyes. Quaoar wished it a good journey to his ancestor's lands. It was a solemn moment and Quaoar respected the fallen creature.

Quaoar hefted his stone knife and walked to where the bear had fallen. He was happy to have made his shot as the bear would provide for his family for many months. He laid out his traveling skins to handle the fat and meat. He worked as coyotes began to circle. He filleted the meat into strips with salt and packed as much fat as he could carry. Quaoar removed the bearskin. His parents Tacu and Auzar would appreciate new coats.

Coyotes, known as Tukupar Itar; the Sky Coyote, approached. Quaoar wished them good eating. It was never a good idea to slight the coyotes. They were famous tricksters and prone to evil pranks as was their nature. The path led up to the deep canyon where his dried grass and twigs shelter was. He longed for his wife who was in their seasonal home along the coast.

The day grew dark so Quaoar made ready to hoist his meat pack up a tree to protect against the coyotes and possums. He used his sparking tool to build a small fire in a cleared area and laid down for a restless sleep.

The sun rose out of the east over the mountain range and Quaoar took his sighting stone out. He used the stone for navigation to sight stars to mark his way home. After he collected his pack and the bear pack, he made way along the river trail. It was two full days of travel to reach the coast and he needed to get the bear meat home for curing.

Quaoar could sense something on the edge of his vision as he traveled and caught a glimpse of Tukupar Itar. One coyote was making the journey with him. Quaoar took one chunk of salted bear meat out and left it along the path. Better to appease the gods of Tukupar Itar. Quaoar settled along the riverbank of the and made preparations for the night. Tukupar Itar also laid down in view of Quaoar’s fire. Quaoar understood the Tukupar Itar's need for companionship. The coyote crept closer until he was across the fire from Quaoar. The Indian could see that the coyote had injured his rear hindquarters had a cactus thorn. Quaoar crept closer and with a quick movement, removed the thorn. The coyote jumped in pain and snarled. “Tukupar Itar, I mean you no harm. Please stay as long as you like.” Since he didn't exactly trust the creature, Quaoar slept with one eye open until the sun arose. The coyote was drinking from the river and came back to sit by the fire.

Quaoar made his way toward the coast thinking of his wife and dog and his parents. Tukupar Itar also walked alongside him. “How am I going to explain bringing a coyote into the upper Mesa?” The coyote looked at him as if to say, “you'll figure it out. I'm staying.”

The day dawned clear and windy which was a sign they were nearing the coast. The river trail turned north into the coastal valley and he could see the glint off the ocean waters. A shout hailed him “Quaoar returns! Tujunga, your husband is near!” His parents came out of their hut and he heard a bark. Running to greet him with joy was his dog and his wife. “Wishmenokre!” Tujunga said. “I love you so much!”

Quaoar hugged her and said “I've brought you a new coat and a new dog. Tukupar Itar looked out for me and I helped him.” He looked back and saw his dog and Tukupar Itar greeting each other. “My parents, I have missed you also. The sighting stone did indeed help my travels. Thank you for loaning it to me.”

Later that day, his family held a big feast to honor Quaoar, He gazed upon his parents, his wife, and his dogs. It's been a good day for everyone!

Wiishmenokre is Tongvan for I love you! The story of the Tongva/Gabrielino Indians goes back almost 8000 years. Spanish missionaries enslaved the Tongva and their culture was eradicated. Little remains of their culture and language. A thousand words of their words and songs from wax cylinder recordings remain. The sighting stone that Quaoar used is a real stone but its actual uses are unknown. Since it has very little wear, archeologists thought that it was for ceremonial uses.

The wetlands where they made their home still echoes the sound of the Tukupar Itar. Residents hear the sounds of Quaoar returning with his Tukupar Itar.
